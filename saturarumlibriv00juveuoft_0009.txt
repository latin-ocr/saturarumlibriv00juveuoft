in doubt.¹ The priinary meaning of the word is ‘ sated,'

drink,² but its use as the designation of a literary type may
perhaps be explained by means of the expressions per satu-
ram, lana· satura, and lex satura.³ In these phrases, with
the idea of fulness there was developed the further notion
of mixture or variety ; for per saturam means ‘ confusedly,'
‘without orderly a1·1·angement,’ lana: satura is a dish filled
with various ingredients, and lex satura a law embodying a
number of different provisions.⁴ The most prominent idea,
then, in this early use of the word satura is that of a medley
or mixture, and this seems to have been in the mind of
Juvenal when he spoke of his own work as a hotch—potch.⁸
